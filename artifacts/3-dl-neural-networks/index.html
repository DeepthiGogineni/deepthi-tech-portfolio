Introduction

This artifact was created for Workshop 3 in the AI and Machine Learning course at Indiana Wesleyan University. Deep learning powers the most visible AI applications today, from facial recognition to language translation to generative art. But behind these applications are distinct neural network architectures, each designed for a specific type of problem. This artifact breaks down the four foundational architectures and explains how neural networks actually learn, giving readers the practical knowledge to evaluate and apply these technologies in real settings.

Description

A technical analysis covering four major neural network architectures: Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) for structured data tasks like classification and regression, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for visual data including image recognition and object detection, Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and their LSTM variants for sequential data like language and time series, and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) for synthetic data generation. The artifact also walks through the four-step learning process shared by all neural networks: input processing, weighted connections through hidden layers, activation functions, and backpropagation. An ethics section addresses the deepfake challenge created by GANs.

Objective

To explore the core architectures behind deep learning, understand how neural networks learn from data, and examine the ethical considerations surrounding synthetic data generation and deepfakes.

Process

  1. Identified the four architectures to analyze based on their industry prevalence and distinct problem domains: ANNs for structured data, CNNs for computer vision, RNNs for sequential data, and GANs for generative tasks.

Foundation

How Neural Networks Learn

Neural networks are inspired by the human brain. They consist of interconnected layers of nodes that process data, recognize patterns, and improve their accuracy over time through a structured learning process.

1

Input Layer

Raw data enters the network through the input layer. Each node receives a piece of the data, which could be pixel values from an image, words from a sentence, or numbers from a spreadsheet.

2

Hidden Layers and Weights

Data passes through hidden layers where each connection carries a weight. These weights determine how much influence one node has on the next.

The network adjusts these weights during training to improve its predictions. Deeper networks can learn more complex patterns.

3

Activation Functions

Activation functions decide whether a node should fire or stay silent. Common functions include ReLU, Sigmoid, and Tanh.

Without activation functions, a neural network would only learn simple linear relationships, limiting its ability to solve complex problems.

4

Backpropagation

After making a prediction, the network calculates the error by comparing it to the correct answer. Backpropagation sends this error backward through the network, adjusting weights layer by layer.

This cycle repeats thousands of times until the model reaches an acceptable level of accuracy.

Architectures

Types of Neural Networks

ANN

Artificial Neural Networks

ANNs are the foundation for all other architectures. They consist of an input layer, one or more hidden layers, and an output layer, with data flowing in one direction from input to output.

  • Best suited for structured, tabular data
  • Used for classification and regression tasks
  • Easier to train and interpret than deeper models
  • Applications include credit scoring, customer churn prediction, and medical diagnosis
CNN

Convolutional Neural Networks

CNNs are designed specifically for processing visual data. They use convolutional layers that scan an image with small filters to detect features like edges, textures, and shapes.

These detected features build up to more complex patterns in deeper layers, allowing the network to recognize full objects from simple components.

  • Excels at image recognition and object detection
  • Uses pooling layers to reduce data size while keeping key features
  • AlexNet winning ImageNet in 2012 proved their power
  • Applications include facial recognition, medical imaging, and self-driving cars
RNN

Recurrent Neural Networks

RNNs are built to handle sequential data where the order matters. Unlike standard neural networks, RNNs have loops that allow information from previous steps to persist.

This gives the network a form of memory, enabling it to understand context and relationships across time steps in the data.

  • Processes data in sequences, one step at a time
  • Handles variable-length inputs naturally
  • LSTM and GRU variants solve the vanishing gradient problem
  • Applications include language translation, speech recognition, and stock price forecasting
GAN

Generative Adversarial Networks

GANs consist of two networks competing against each other. The Generator creates synthetic data, while the Discriminator tries to distinguish real data from fake.

Through this competition, the Generator learns to produce increasingly realistic outputs over time.

  • Introduced by Ian Goodfellow in 2014
  • Generates realistic images, audio, and video
  • Used in data augmentation when training data is limited
  • Applications include art generation, image enhancement, and drug discovery
Responsibility

Ethical Considerations

The Deepfake Challenge

The same GAN technology that enables creative applications also raises serious ethical concerns. Deepfakes can produce highly convincing fake videos, images, and audio that are increasingly difficult to distinguish from real content.

Understanding these risks is just as important as understanding the technology itself. Responsible AI development means considering how these tools can be misused and what safeguards should be in place.

  • Deepfakes can spread misinformation and manipulate public opinion through fabricated video or audio content
  • Identity fraud risks increase as face and voice synthesis become more realistic and accessible
  • Organizations need clear policies for disclosing AI-generated content to maintain trust
  • Detection tools and digital watermarking are emerging as countermeasures, but they remain an ongoing challenge
  • Regulations around synthetic media are still evolving, making ethical self-governance essential for AI professionals

Conclusion

Each neural network architecture is designed to solve a different category of problems. ANNs handle structured data, CNNs process visual information, RNNs understand sequences, and GANs generate entirely new data. As these networks become more capable, building AI solutions that are both effective and ethical requires understanding not just how these systems work, but also how they impact the world around us.
