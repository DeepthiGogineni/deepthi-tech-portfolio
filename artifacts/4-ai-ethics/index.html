AI Ethics & Responsible AI

An analysis of the ethical challenges facing AI today, covering bias, privacy, transparency, and accountability through real-world case studies and enterprise applications.

Introduction

This artifact was created for Workshop 4 in the AI and Machine Learning course at Indiana Wesleyan University. As AI becomes embedded in hiring, lending, healthcare, and law enforcement, the ethical stakes of getting it wrong have never been higher. This artifact examines the four core pillars of responsible AI and shows, through real incidents, what happens when organizations ignore them. For professionals deploying AI in enterprise environments, understanding these risks is not optional.

Description

An analysis structured around four pillars of responsible AI: Bias and Fairness, covering Amazon's discriminatory hiring tool and racial disparities in facial recognition accuracy; Data Privacy, examining the Cambridge Analytica incident and regulations like GDPR and CCPA; Transparency and Explainability, analyzing the COMPAS recidivism scoring tool and techniques like SHAP and LIME; and Accountability, addressing autonomous vehicle incidents and the importance of human-in-the-loop systems. Three real-world case studies ground each concept in healthcare (IBM Watson Oncology), finance (Apple Card gender bias), and enterprise platforms (Salesforce Trusted AI).

Objective

To identify and analyze the core ethical challenges in AI development, including algorithmic bias, data privacy, model transparency, and accountability in automated decision-making.

Process

  1. Identified four ethical pillars based on the most commonly cited risks in enterprise AI deployment: bias and fairness, data privacy, transparency and explainability, and accountability.
  2. Researched real incidents for each pillar, selecting cases with documented outcomes and public records. Amazon hiring tool (2018), Apple Card gender bias (2019), COMPAS recidivism scoring, and IBM Watson Oncology were selected for their measurable impact and industry relevance.
  3. Analyzed each incident using the pillar framework to identify what failed, who was harmed, and which mitigation approach would have reduced the risk.
  4. Connected each pillar to its regulatory and technical response: GDPR and CCPA for privacy, SHAP and LIME for transparency, human-in-the-loop design for accountability, and fairness-aware algorithms for bias.
  5. Mapped all findings to Salesforce's Trusted AI framework to show how these ethical principles translate directly into enterprise AI deployment decisions on a platform used by 150,000 organizations.

Tools

  • Published incident reports (Reuters, IEEE proceedings) -- chosen because primary documentation of real incidents is more credible and verifiable than secondary summaries or opinion pieces.
  • EU AI Act regulatory text (2024) -- used because it represents the current legal benchmark for AI governance in high-risk applications, directly relevant to any organization deploying AI in Europe or globally.
  • Salesforce Trusted AI documentation -- included specifically because the portfolio targets Salesforce ecosystem professionals who will deploy these ethical principles on a platform they use daily.
  • Gender Shades research (Buolamwini and Gebru, 2018) -- selected as the most rigorous academic study measuring real bias disparity with a controlled methodology, providing a concrete example of how to audit AI systems for fairness.

Value Proposition

Shows the ability to evaluate AI systems beyond technical performance and assess their societal impact. This is a critical skill for professionals who deploy AI in healthcare, finance, hiring, and customer-facing platforms.

Unique Value

Most AI portfolios focus only on building models. This artifact demonstrates the ability to think critically about what happens after a model is deployed, who it affects, and what safeguards are needed to prevent harm.

Relevance

Salesforce has built its entire AI strategy around Trusted AI principles. Understanding bias detection, data privacy, and transparent AI directly connects to how Einstein features are designed, deployed, and governed in enterprise environments.

Core Pillars

Four Pillars of Responsible AI

Pillar 1

Bias and Fairness

If training data contains biases, the model will reproduce and amplify them, leading to unfair outcomes in hiring, lending, and healthcare.

  • Amazon scrapped an AI hiring tool that penalized resumes containing the word "women's"
  • Facial recognition systems show higher error rates for people with darker skin tones
  • Mitigation requires diverse datasets, regular audits, and fairness-aware algorithms
Pillar 2

Data Privacy

AI systems need large amounts of personal data to train. Without proper consent and safeguards, this creates serious privacy risks for individuals.

  • The Cambridge Analytica scandal exposed how personal data can be misused at scale
  • GDPR and CCPA require explicit consent and the right to data deletion
  • Techniques like differential privacy and federated learning help protect user data
Pillar 3

Transparency and Explainability

Many AI models operate as black boxes. In high-stakes areas like healthcare or criminal sentencing, people have a right to understand how decisions are made.

  • The COMPAS recidivism tool was criticized for unexplainable risk scores in sentencing
  • Explainable AI (XAI) methods like SHAP and LIME help interpret model decisions
  • The EU AI Act requires transparency for high-risk AI systems
Pillar 4

Accountability

When AI causes harm, there must be clear ownership. Without accountability, organizations can deflect blame onto algorithms.

  • Self-driving car incidents raised questions about who is liable when AI fails
  • Human-in-the-loop systems ensure people can override automated decisions
  • Regular model monitoring and audit trails are essential for accountability
Real-World Impact

Case Studies

1

Healthcare: IBM Watson Oncology

IBM Watson was marketed as a cancer treatment advisor but was found to sometimes recommend unsafe treatments. It was trained on synthetic cases rather than real patient data, raising concerns about AI in life-critical decisions.

2

Finance: Apple Card Gender Bias

In 2019, Apple Card offered significantly lower credit limits to women than men with similar financial profiles. This showed how AI can produce discriminatory outcomes through hidden correlations in training data, even without gender as an input.

3

Enterprise: Salesforce Trusted AI

Salesforce embeds ethical AI principles through its Trusted AI framework. Einstein includes bias detection, transparency reports, and data governance controls that help organizations deploy AI responsibly.

Conclusion

Building AI that works is only half the challenge. Making it fair, private, transparent, and accountable is what makes it trustworthy. Professionals who understand these ethical dimensions will be the ones who build systems that organizations and users can genuinely rely on.

Reflection

Artifact Reflection

Audience Customization

This artifact was designed for students and professionals who need to understand the ethical responsibilities of deploying AI. Each pillar is supported with real incidents that show the consequences of ignoring ethics.

Lessons Learned

This artifact made me realize that technical accuracy alone does not make an AI system good. A model can perform well but still cause harm if it is biased or deployed without oversight. I also learned how platforms like Salesforce are actively building ethics into their AI tools.

Feedback Received

Peers suggested adding more real-world examples to make ethical concepts feel practical rather than theoretical. They also recommended connecting each pillar to specific industries where the impact is most visible.

Revisions Applied

Based on the feedback, I added case studies from healthcare, finance, and enterprise platforms to ground each ethical pillar in real incidents. The four-pillar structure was refined to give each challenge its own distinct focus rather than grouping them together.
